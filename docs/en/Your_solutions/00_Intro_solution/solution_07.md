# Logic & Relative Motion: The Fly and the Bicycle

This document provides a solution to the classic "Fly and Two Trains" (or in this case, a bicycle and a wall) problem using a logical approach rather than complex infinite series.

## Problem Statement

A bicycle is **10 meters** from a wall and moves toward it at a constant speed of **$1\text{ m/s}$**. A fly starts from the bicycle and flies toward the wall at **$2\text{ m/s}$**. The fly constantly bounces between the bicycle and the wall until the bicycle hits the wall.



**Goal:** Calculate the total distance the fly travels before the bicycle reaches the wall.

---

## Step 1: Calculate the Time to Impact

Instead of tracking each individual "trip" the fly makes, we first determine how long the entire process lasts. The process ends when the bicycle hits the wall.

Given:
- Initial Distance ($d$) = $10\text{ meters}$
- Speed of Bicycle ($v_b$) = $1\text{ m/s}$

The time ($t$) taken for the bicycle to reach the wall is:

$$
t = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}}
$$

$$
t = \frac{10\text{ m}}{1\text{ m/s}} = 10\text{ seconds}
$$

---

## Step 2: Calculate the Fly's Total Distance

Since the fly is flying at a **constant speed** for the entire duration of the bicycle's trip, we don't need to care about how many times it turns around. We only need its speed and the total time it was in the air.

Given:
- Speed of Fly ($v_f$) = $2\text{ m/s}$
- Total Time ($t$) = $10\text{ seconds}$

The total distance ($d_f$) traveled by the fly is:

$$
d_f = \text{Speed of Fly} \times \text{Total Time}
$$

$$
d_f = 2\text{ m/s} \times 10\text{ s}
$$

$$
d_f = 20\text{ meters}
$$

---

## Final Solution

The fly travels a total distance of:

$$
20\text{ meters}
$$

### Summary
- **Time Elapsed:** $10\text{ seconds}$
- **Fly's Speed:** $2\text{ m/s}$
- **Logic:** Distance = Speed $\times$ Time. Because the fly's speed is constant, the number of turns does not change the total distance calculation.
