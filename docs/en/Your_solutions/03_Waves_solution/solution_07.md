# Standing Waves: Calculating Wavelength from Antinodes

## Problem Statement
A standing wave with **four antinodes** is produced on a string of length **L = 80 cm**. What is the wavelength of this wave?

---

## Step-by-Step Solution

This problem is highly visual and doesn't require complex formulas if we use the "jump rope" logic.

### Step 1: Translate the Keywords
* **String Length ($L$):** 80 cm (or 0.8 meters)
* **Antinodes:** 4
    
*What is an antinode?* Think of an antinode as one big "loop" or "bump" on a vibrating string. In physics, **one loop is exactly half of a full wavelength ($\lambda/2$)**. 

### Step 2: Build the Logic
If one loop is half a wave, then two loops make one full wave ($\lambda$).
Since this string has **4 loops** (antinodes), it means there are exactly **2 full waves** fitting across the entire length of the string.

We can write this as a simple logic equation: 
*Length of String = 2 Full Waves*
$$L = 2 \cdot \lambda$$

### Step 3: Solve for Wavelength ($\lambda$)
Now we just plug in our given string length (80 cm) and solve for the wavelength.
$$80 = 2 \cdot \lambda$$
    
Divide both sides by 2 to isolate $\lambda$:
$$\lambda = \frac{80}{2}$$
$$\lambda = 40 \text{ cm}$$

*(Note: In physics, it is always a good habit to convert your final answer to standard SI units. To convert centimeters to meters, divide by 100.)*
* $40 \text{ cm} = 0.4 \text{ m}$

**Final Answer:**
The wavelength of this standing wave is **40 cm** (or **0.4 meters**).
